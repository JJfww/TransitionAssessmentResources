### Getting to Know Ourselves and Each Other

Young adults will assess their individual learning styles, personal boundaries, personal preferences, and community interests as they participate in various activities throughout the month. The concept of team building will be introduced to the young adults as they participate in “icebreaker activities”. Person centered planning will be (re)visited as the young adults review, revise, or make their goals and help develop their individual schedules.

##### Suggested community outings/visitors (if you need ideas):

- Ride the bus! Take the bus all around town and make it a scavenger hunt. Take the full route from a pickup place (that is, get on and off at the same place) and have students note places they see that they would like to visit. 
  
  - You can extend this to a communication activity to tell the class/small group what/where/why. 

- Have all students write a place they'd like to visit on a piece of paper (within reason - hours/location feasible within the school day). Have a contest to see who gets to draw the place, and then collaboriatively plan the trip (bus, times, what to do)

[Person Centered Planning tools](https://www.lifecoursetools.com/lifecourse-library/foundational-tools/person-centered/) directs you to the LifeCourse Library's section on Person-Centered Foundational Tools. LifeCourse Library is a resource hub that offers tools, materials, and resources to support individuals with disabilities and their families in achieving their desired outcomes across the lifespan.

The Person-Centered Foundational Tools are designed to help individuals with disabilities, their families, and professionals create person-centered plans that take into account the individual's strengths, preferences, and needs. These tools are meant to empower people to take control of their lives and to ensure that services and supports are tailored to meet their unique requirements.

The tools in this section are organized into the following categories:

1. **Charting the LifeCourse Framework**: A collection of tools and resources to help individuals and families develop a vision for a good life, identify the steps needed to achieve it, and understand the different types of supports available.

2. **Person-Centered Thinking and Planning**: Tools and resources that support the use of person-centered thinking and planning processes, which focus on the individual's unique strengths, preferences, and needs.

3. **Conversations on the Journey**: Resources to help facilitate conversations about the person's life journey, including their past experiences, present situation, and future goals.

4. **Implementation and Monitoring**: Tools to support the implementation of person-centered plans and monitor progress toward desired outcomes.

5. **Capacity Building**: Resources to help individuals, families, and professionals develop the skills and knowledge needed to create and sustain person-centered plans and supports.

[Circles of Friends/Support](https://inclusion.com/path-maps-and-person-centered-planning/circles-friends-2/) is a person-centered approach to building support networks for individuals with disabilities. This   platform offers resources and information on inclusive practices, person-centered planning, and community building for people with disabilities.

Circles of Friends is a strategy that involves creating a support network of friends, family, professionals, and community members who come together to help an individual with disabilities achieve their goals and aspirations. The main purpose of Circles of Friends is to promote social inclusion and ensure that individuals with disabilities have the opportunity to build meaningful relationships and participate fully in their community.

The page provides the following information on Circles of Friends:

1. **Introduction**: A brief explanation of the Circles of Friends concept and its benefits.
2. **Stories and Examples**: Real-life stories and examples of how Circles of Friends have positively impacted the lives of individuals with disabilities.
3. **Articles and Publications**: A list of articles and publications that discuss the Circles of Friends approach and provide guidance on how to implement it.
4. **Workshops and Training**: Information on workshops and training sessions offered by Inclusion.com to help families, educators, and support workers develop the skills and knowledge needed to create and maintain Circles of Friends.
5. **Videos**: A selection of videos showcasing stories of Circles of Friends and the positive impact they have on the lives of individuals with disabilities.

### Optional Icebreaker Activities

Modify/adapt to meet your students' needs. Include your paras in the activities! 

1. Sensory scavenger hunt: Create a list of items with different sensory characteristics (e.g., soft, smooth, rough, warm, cold) that can be found in the environment. Participants work in pairs or small groups to find and collect these items. This activity encourages communication and teamwork while engaging various senses.
2. The name game: Each participant introduces themselves and shares a word or phrase that starts with the same letter as their name (e.g., "Friendly Fred"). As each person introduces themselves, the group must repeat the previous names and adjectives. This activity helps participants remember each other's names and encourages listening skills.
3. Collaborative art project: Participants work together to create a group art piece, such as a mural or collage. Encourage them to communicate their ideas and preferences, either verbally or non-verbally. This activity fosters teamwork, creativity, and self-expression.
4. Two truths and a lie: Each participant shares three statements about themselves – two truths and one lie. The rest of the group tries to guess which statement is the lie. This activity encourages sharing and helps participants learn more about each other.
5. Would you rather: Participants take turns asking "Would you rather" questions (e.g., "Would you rather be able to fly or be invisible?"). Everyone answers the question, and it can spark interesting discussions and help the group learn more about each other's preferences.
6. Common ground: Participants are given a list of topics (e.g., favorite movies, hobbies, travel destinations) and must find things they have in common with others in the group. This activity encourages communication and helps participants find shared interests.
7. Find someone who: Create a list of statements such as "Find someone who has a pet," "Find someone who has traveled to another country," or "Find someone who plays a musical instrument." Participants must find someone in the group who matches each statement and write their names down. This activity encourages mingling and conversation.
